Weeknotes & Learning in the Open
==================================

## What is this?

Weeknotes are short, regular updates about what you're doing, what you're learning, and what's not going well. They're a habit — not a publication. They don't need to be polished.

The practice of "working in the open" means documenting your journey as you go, so others can learn from it — including your mistakes.

## Why bother?

- **For you**: Writing things down clarifies your thinking. Looking back over a few months of weeknotes is surprisingly useful.
- **For your community**: People can see what's happening without having to ask. It builds trust.
- **For others**: Other neighbourhoods, practices, and communities trying to do similar things can learn from your experience instead of starting from zero.
- **For the project**: The weeknotes become an honest record. When you want to write a report, a blog post, or a funding bid — the raw material is already there.

## The weeknotes convention

### Who writes them?

Anyone can. Ideally a few different people take turns so it's not a burden and you get different perspectives.

### How often?

Weekly or fortnightly. Whatever you can sustain. Regularity matters more than length.

### How long?

A few paragraphs is fine. Three bullet points is fine. A single sentence is better than nothing.

### What goes in them?

A weeknote typically has three parts:

**What happened** — what did we do this week? Meetings, events, decisions, things that launched, things that broke.

**What we learned** — what surprised us? What did we find out? What changed our thinking?

**What's stuck** — what isn't working? Where are we blocked? What are we unsure about?

You can also add:

**Shoutouts** — someone did something great? Say so.

**Links** — anything useful you found or want to share.

### Template

```
## Weeknotes — [date range] — [your name]

### What happened
-

### What we learned
-

### What's stuck
-

### Shoutouts
-

### Links
-
```

### Where do they go?

Post them in the **Weeknotes** category on the forum. This category is public so anyone can read them.

Tag them with whatever's relevant: `what-happened`, `what-we-learned`, `whats-stuck`, `shoutout`.

## Sharing outputs

When you've produced something concrete — a guide, a template, a report, a set of recommendations, a presentation — post it in the **Outputs & Resources** category.

This is the "finished" counterpart to weeknotes. Weeknotes are the journey; outputs are the destination.

### What counts as an output?

Anything someone else could use or learn from:

- A guide to setting up a community forum
- A summary of what you learned from talking to residents
- A template for community guidelines
- A presentation you gave at an event
- A report for a funder
- A list of local services or resources
- A blog post published elsewhere (link to it)

### How to share

Post in the Outputs & Resources category with:

- A short summary of what it is and who it's for
- The thing itself (uploaded document, link, or written directly in the post)
- Tag it: `guide`, `template`, `report`, `presentation`, `link`

## From private reflection to public output

Some learning happens in honest, members-only conversations — retrospectives, candid feedback, things that went wrong. That's fine and important. Keep those in the members-only Retrospectives category.

But the learning from those conversations often deserves to be shared more widely. The flow is:

1. **Have the honest conversation** in Retrospectives (members-only)
2. **Distill the learning** — what's the takeaway that would help others?
3. **Share the distilled version** in Weeknotes or Outputs (public)

The raw, candid discussion stays private. The useful learning goes public. This way people feel safe being honest AND the community contributes to the wider commons.
